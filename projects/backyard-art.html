Backyard Art

Responsive, Web Design

Backyard Art is a web application I made for my final project at the Iron Yard. Every page is completely responsive and was created through the use of HTML5, SASS, and jQuery plugins.

The website tackles Austin's lack of local online resources for artists and art lovers who live here. More specifically, it provides a platform for artists to share and/or sell their artwork, see what other artists in town are creating with the ability to contact them for collaboration purposes, and for people wanting to "Keep Austin Weird" and buy art locally.

It also has a calendar with every art event occurring in Austin, including: festival dates and submission deadlines, art exhibit and gallery openings, art related meetups, classes, and so much more.

Research

I started my research by conducting interviews with potential users of the site that were both artists and art buyers. Through their responses I was able to have a better understanding of what features the site should have. I also researched the structure of other art sites in regards to sorting and displaying a lot of art images. By combining those results I was able to optimize the page layouts and search options to better accommodate the users needs.

I created user personas to help give me better sense of my target audience and the characteristics/interests that they would have. I honed in on three main audiences: artists that are struggling to get their art seen and sold, people interested in buying art from local artists, and artists that are recent Austin transplants and want to get involved in the art community.

Then I broke down all of the strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats that my site would have. I focused not only on current aspects of the site, but more importantly on future possibilities and features it could have if it were to continue beyond being just my final project.

Making wordlists is a huge part of my research process in creating a design direction. Through listing various words that my mind connects to the website into categories (people, places, things, adjectives, and colors) I'm able to group together similar words to form my possible design themes.

The Design

For the initial design process, I drew up sketches of each page for small, medium, and large screen sizes then I implemented them into wireframes using Adobe Illustrator.

The design direction I decided to go in was based off of my first style tile which is centered around the idea of how the world is viewed in black and white but art is the pop of color that brings it to life. For an overview of all my style tile themes, visit my style tile concepts page.

The background images were taken by Louis Lander Deacon (Imagine Dragons' Head Me album cover photographer) and were chosen for their beauty and relevance to the website's name due to being taken outdoors. Through the use of CSS animation I switched between black and white to colored versions of 3 photos from his collection to correlate with my design theme.

I selected Oswald Regular for my headers due to its modern sans-serif appearance and for its legibility on images when used in all uppercase. I paired it with another modern sans-serif, Poppins Light, for its more rounded edges and contrast next to Oswald's angular features.

Inline popup

It was important to me that all of the content on the site be genuine to my concept. All of the art featured on the artist directory page is from actual Austin artists who I'm friends with on Instagram that gave me permission to use their art. The art featured Ramona Flowers' page is by my favorite artist, Steven Vigil, but since he doesn't live in Austin he agreed to letting me use a character from one of my favorite movies as the artist.

Logo and Icons

What I enjoyed most was using Adobe Illustrator to create a logo for my site and icons for three reoccurring meetups on my events calendar.

The logo was created to symbolize the art factor through an artist's beret, and represent Austin by using a cut out of the shape of Texas and a mustache since facial hair is a very common feature amongst males living here.

For the Lifedrawing meetup I decided to reenact an actual life drawing by creating a simple sketch/outline of a female figure. With sketch bomb I took their name literally by using pencils as if they were TNT. Being that I attend the Art Attacks Austin meetup, I tried to encompass their fun and quirky audience through making a colorful warrior unicorn whose horn is "attacking" Austin.

Challenges

Finding a calendar tool to use was particularly difficult. It was crucial that it could hold a lot of content, display that content in an aesthetically pleasing way, and run smoothly for the user. I decided to use UpTo for its responsive design, customization, simple iframe implementation, and easy to use site maneuverability. Due to time constraints, the site's current calendar contains a few example events from every category in order to give an idea of how the real calendar would look.

I faced another challenge during the initial design stage when creating my color palette. I wanted it to reflect my "pop of color vs. black and white" theme, yet not detract any attention from the most important elements on the page: the art. I decided on using only one color, a vibrant blue/turquoise that I pulled from the background image on the homepage, and used it sparingly on only the most important text or as an accent color in order to draw just enough attention.

The biggest challenges I faced were creating a grid layout on the individual artist's page and optimizing all of the images. I wanted the layout to be clean and cohesive, but the images' different sizing made it difficult. One of the most important things when it comes to displaying art with the intention of selling it is that you cannot alter or crop the photos unless given permission by the artist. The only exception is with thumbnails when they're used as a representation of the art and are a means to take the user to the actual art image/page. I avoided having to crop the photos by by choosing an appropriate layout to fit fit their varying dimensions. Image optimization was an issue because I wanted the site to have a fast loading time/low data usage for the user, but at the same time I didn't want to reduce the art's quality and details in the photos by shrinking them down. I was able solve this issue using tools in Adobe Photoshop that allowed me to reduce the file size of the image without drastically affecting its resolution.
